Voyager · UX Design Spec · Step 9 · Design Directions

Four design directions, one register.

All four directions inherit the AiRT-class typographic and color foundation locked in Steps 5–8 (JetBrains Mono / Source Serif 4 / Inter; deep-space palette; warm-gold functional accent). What varies between them is execution at the level finer than tokens: chapter marker shape, scrubber stroke, HUD grouping and density, and how the chapter copy panel rests.

Each direction is shown across two states: cruise (idle mid-mission, no encounter active) and encounter (V1 Jupiter, with the detail scrubber expanded and the narrow-angle cone aimed at Io). The encounter view is where the differentiator lives, so it's the more diagnostic comparison.

Comparison at a glance

A — Mission Control is the most legible at first glance but pays for that legibility with chrome that competes with the canvas. The accent-gold borders and labeled pins read as designed surfaces, not as restraint.

B — AiRT Canonical is the most disciplined application of the Step 5 reference set. Distributed HUD as pure typography over the canvas; hairline scrubber; markers as quiet dots. The risk is discoverability: Maya may not notice the chapter markers without prompting. Mitigated by hover-affordance and chapter-marker focus-ring on keyboard navigation.

C — Cinema is the most restrained. Minimal chrome; the simulation does all the talking. Loses some affordance density (instrument-shutoff status becomes hard to surface; provenance labels need a different home). Closest to watching a silent film of the mission.

D — Editorial pulls the NYT long-scroll register forward. The always-visible chapter copy strip means the prose competes more with the canvas; the bottom-centered HUD is unusual but reads as editorial caption. Strongest for Marcus (the classroom case) and weakest for the "canvas is the protagonist" commitment.

The recommendation is Direction B. It maximally inherits the AiRT register established in Step 5, leans into typography as the primary UI surface, and keeps the canvas as the protagonist. The discoverability risk (markers too quiet) is addressable through hover/focus affordance design without altering the resting state. It is the direction whose mistakes are the most fixable.
